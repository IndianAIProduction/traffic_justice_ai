STATE-SPECIFIC TRAFFIC RULES AND DIFFERENCES IN INDIA
(Under the Motor Vehicles (Amendment) Act, 2019)

OVERVIEW OF STATE ADOPTION

After the Motor Vehicles (Amendment) Act, 2019 was passed by Parliament on 31 July 2019 and came into effect on 1 September 2019, Indian states responded differently to the new penalty structure. The central government sets the maximum penalties, and states have the authority to reduce (but not increase) fine amounts through state government notifications.

CATEGORY 1 — STATES WITH FULL CENTRAL RATES

The following states and Union Territories adopted the full central penalty schedule without reductions:
- Delhi (Union Territory)
- Chandigarh (Union Territory)
- Haryana (among the first states to adopt)
- Karnataka
- Maharashtra (largely central with minor modifications)
- Bihar
- Uttarakhand
- Jharkhand (largely central)
- Andhra Pradesh (largely central)
- Telangana (advanced eChallan system)
- Himachal Pradesh
- Assam
- Jammu and Kashmir (post Article 370 reorganisation)

CATEGORY 2 — STATES WITH REDUCED FINES

The following states adopted reduced fines from the central schedule:

GUJARAT — Among the most significant reductions nationwide. Initially adopted full central rates in September 2019, but faced massive public protests and rolled back fines within weeks via state notification dated 17 October 2019. Key reductions include:
- Dangerous driving: Rs 5,000 reduced to Rs 1,500
- Drunken driving: Rs 10,000 reduced to Rs 2,000
- Driving without licence: Rs 5,000 reduced to Rs 2,000
- Not wearing helmet: Rs 1,000 reduced to Rs 500
- Not wearing seatbelt: Rs 1,000 reduced to Rs 500
- Over-speeding: Rs 2,000 reduced to Rs 1,500

UTTAR PRADESH — Significant reductions via state notification dated 9 December 2019. Key reductions include:
- General offenses: Rs 500 reduced to Rs 250
- Over-speeding: Rs 2,000 reduced to Rs 400
- Dangerous driving: Rs 5,000 reduced to Rs 2,500
- Drunken driving: Rs 10,000 reduced to Rs 2,500
- Not wearing helmet: Rs 1,000 reduced to Rs 500
- Not wearing seatbelt: Rs 1,000 reduced to Rs 500

RAJASTHAN — Moderate reductions across most categories.

TAMIL NADU — Delayed implementation (effective approximately April 2020) with moderate reductions.

MADHYA PRADESH — Moderate reductions, particularly for common violations.

PUNJAB — Delayed adoption with significant reductions across multiple categories.

ODISHA — Moderate reductions, especially for helmet and seatbelt violations.

GOA — Reduced fines, particularly for common tourist-area violations.

CHHATTISGARH — Delayed implementation with moderate reductions.

KERALA — Partial adoption with selective reductions. Kerala has one of India's highest road accident rates and maintains relatively strict enforcement despite some fine reductions.

CATEGORY 3 — STATES THAT RESISTED IMPLEMENTATION

WEST BENGAL — The most prominent example of resistance. Chief Minister Mamata Banerjee publicly refused to implement the hiked central fines. The state continued to enforce pre-2019 amendment rates, which are significantly lower:
- Not wearing helmet: Rs 100 (vs central Rs 1,000)
- Driving without licence: Rs 500 (vs central Rs 5,000)
- Dangerous driving: Rs 1,000 (vs central Rs 5,000)
- Drunken driving: Rs 2,000 (vs central Rs 10,000)
- Not wearing seatbelt: Rs 100 (vs central Rs 1,000)

IMPORTANT NOTES FOR CITIZENS

1. KNOW YOUR STATE'S RATES: The fine you should pay depends on which state the violation occurs in, not where your vehicle is registered. For example, if you have a Gujarat-registered vehicle but are caught in Delhi, Delhi's fine rates apply.

2. ALWAYS ASK FOR AN OFFICIAL RECEIPT: Regardless of the state, you are entitled to an official printed receipt (eChallan or written challan). Verbal fines and cash collection without receipt are illegal.

3. FINE VARIATIONS BY VEHICLE TYPE: Some sections have different rates for Light Motor Vehicles (LMV) vs Medium/Heavy Goods Vehicles (M/HGV). Section 183 (speeding) has higher fines for heavy vehicles.

4. REPEAT OFFENSE HIGHER RATES: Most sections have higher fines for repeat or subsequent offenses. The repeat offense rate typically applies if the previous conviction was within the last three years.

5. IMPRISONMENT PROVISIONS: Several offenses carry imprisonment in addition to or instead of fines, including:
   - Dangerous driving (Section 184): Up to 6 months first offense, 2 years repeat
   - Drunken driving (Section 185): Up to 6 months first offense, 2 years repeat
   - Driving while disqualified (Section 182): Up to 3 months
   - Hit and run (Section 187): Up to 6 months to 2 years
   - Juvenile offenses (Section 199): Up to 3 years for guardian/owner

6. LICENSE ACTIONS: Some violations trigger licence suspension or cancellation:
   - Not wearing helmet (Section 194B): 3-month licence suspension
   - Juvenile offenses (Section 199): Vehicle registration cancelled
   - Repeat dangerous driving: Licence seizure possible

DRIVER'S RIGHTS ACROSS ALL STATES

Under the Motor Vehicles Act, the following rights apply uniformly across India:

1. Right to know the reason for being stopped
2. Right to see the officer's identification
3. Right to receive an official receipt for any fine paid
4. Right to contest a challan in court (Section 200)
5. Right against abusive language, threats, or physical force
6. Right to record the interaction
7. Right to a written/electronic challan (verbal fines are illegal)
8. Right against arbitrary detention
9. Right to file a complaint against misconduct
10. Right to pay through official channels (not directly to officer)
